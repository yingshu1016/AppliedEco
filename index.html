Abstract¶

Covid-19 crisis has been changing many aspects of individuals' social life globally with the limitation of group gatherings, economic downturns, and the nervousness caused by the fear of getting the virus. We focus our study on a specific group of people - univerisity students - in order to investigate the impact of Covid-19 crisis on the self-assessed studying motivation, group project performance, exam performance, feeling of loneliess, and financial stress, by conducting an online survey. Our results show that the pandemic significantly influences students' studying motivation, feeling of loneliness, and financial stress. Furthermore, we concentrate this research on a subgroup - students in Dutch universities - and we found evidence that the Covid-19 crisis significantly decreases the self-assessed studying movation, group project performance, and increases the feeling of loneliness of the students in Dutch universities.

Research questions¶

In this assignment our aim is to answer the following 5 research questions related to students' life during the Covid-19 crisis:

  1. Does the Covid-19 crisis affect the university students' self-assessed studying motivation ?
  2. Does the Covid-19 crisis affect the university students' self-assessed groupwork performance?
  3. Does the Covid-19 crisis affect the university students' self-assessed exam performance?
  4. Does the Covid-19 crisis affect the univeristy students' self-assessed loneliness ?
  5. Does the Covid-19 crisis affect the university students' self-assessed financial stress?

Motivation¶

Covid-19 crisis has impacted many aspects of the societies, and some might be even profound. Dealing with univerity students, the lockdown prevents face-to-face teaching lessons, and exams are mostly online. In addition, the limitation about the maximum number of people in a group (maximum 2 people in the Netherlands) impedes face-to-face debate, which might reduces the efficiency of group projects' discussions. The lockdown measurements could have psychological impact on students as well. Lastly, many countries are going through economic downturn during this period. For example, the 27 countries of the European Union (EU) suffered from -11.4 % recession in the second quarter of 2020, that is when the Covid-19 was most severe (Eurostat, 2020). The economic downturn could potentially influence the financial stress of univerity students, because they might lose their source of income (i.e. from part-time jobs).

Some studies have already investigated the effects of Covid-19 on university students. In the US, Aucejo et al. (2020) have conducted a survey among 1,500 undergraduate students at Arizona State University in April 2020. They found that 13% of students have delayed graduation, 40% lost a job or an internship, and 29% expect to earn less at the age of 35. Moreover, they pointed out that the effects of Covid-19 pandemic have been highly heterogeneous among university students: in fact, the effect on delayed graduation is 55% more pronounced for lower-income students than higher-income ones. In addition, according to the SMaRteN study (2020), 66% of the 4,000 doctoral candidates who answered the survey, are worried about their future, while 70% of the respondents are facing financial stress. Furthermore, Ambreen and Utsav collected the responses of 500 Indian students, and they found that the 70% is stressed about their studies, and almost 80% of students are not satisfied with online education.

Following this path, we are interested to further investigate the impact of the pandemic on students' self-assessed studying motivation, performance, feeling of loneliness, and financial stress. These factors could potentially impact the long-term development of university students, such as applying post-graduate schools, future earnings, and satisfaction of their future careers.

Data source and dataset¶

First of all, we created a survey with Google Survey in order to address our 5 research questions. The link below contains the Google Spreadsheet with the original data collected.

Students and Covid-19 Survey.

This survey has been completed during the first week of January 2021 by 135 university students. It contains 13 questions about respondents' socio-demographic characteristics: gender, nationality, second nationality, domicile, relationship status, household composition, the attending level of education, major, second major, their university location, their monthly net income before the Covid-19 pandemic, monthly net income after this event, and whether they receive any students' finance or not. Then, we asked students their motivation, group performance, exam performance, loneliness, and financial stress both before and after the Covid-19 pandemic. Respondents were instructed to rank these 5 variables from 1 to 7, with 1 that means "I rank this variable very low", and 7 which stands for "I rank this variable very high". For example: "How do you rank your motivation before the Covid-19 pandemic?". If the respondent's answer is 1, it means that this person believes her motivation was very low before the pandemic. Data cleaning¶

After the data collection, we opened the dataset in Jupiter Notebook, in order to proceed with the data cleaning part. First, we deleted 2 unnecessary columns: "Cronological information" and "Score". These are two columns that Google Survey automatically creates when downloading the results on Google Spreadsheets. Second, we renamed the columns in order to give them shorter and more intuitive names, instead of the entire survey's questions. Third, we removed the Euro currency symbol () from Income_before, Income_after, and Student_finance. Fourth, we checked the answers given by students for the open questions, and we grouped together those with the same meaning but that were formulated with different words. For example, for the variable "Income_before", students with no income answered differently, for example "0", "N.A", "no", "no income", "0 income", and more. Therefore, we grouped all of them and we replaced with the number "0". Lastly, we coded as numeric the following variables: Income_before, Income_after, and Student_finance.

Data analysis (Methodology)¶

We apply Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression models to study the causal impact of Covid-19 on self-assessed outcome variables. Two types of OLS regression models are employed. The models without control variables are written as:

$Y_{i}=\beta_0+\beta_1 Covid_{i}+\epsilon_{i}$

The models with individual background control variables are written as:

$Y_{i}=\beta_0+\beta_1 Covid_{i}+ \beta_2 X_{i} + \epsilon_{i}$

In both equations above, $Y_{i}$ is the self-assessed level of motivation, group project performance, exam performance, loneliness, and financial stress before or during the Covid-19 crisis. $Covid_{i}$ represents whether the observation refers to the pre-Covid period or during the pandemic. The effect of Covid is shown by the coefficient $\beta_1$. In the second equation, $X_{i}$ is a vector of individual background control variables, including gender, nationality, domicile, relationship status, household composition, level of education, major of studying, and the country where the respondent's university is located. $\epsilon_{i}$ is the error term. We compare the results of two types of models for robustness test.

Main assumptions¶

In order to assess the causal impact of Covid-19 on students' outcomes, we have to know the students' outcomes with and without the Covid-19 crisis, mathematically, the potential outcome framework is:

$\Delta(Y)_{i}=Y_{i}(Covid19=1)-Y_{i}(Covid19=0)$

The first term on the right hand side stands for the student i' outcomes with Covid-19.The second term on the right hand side is the student i' outcomes in the state without Covid-19, which is unoberserved.

This study is based on two main assumptions:

1) We use students' self-reported outcomes before the Covid-19 crisis as counterfactual to represent students' outcomes without Covid-19.

2) Our results are fully based on the self-assessed survey. Therefore, another assumption is that students report their outcomes and background information correctly. Especially, we expect they have good understanding of our scales of outcome variables.

The limitation of these assumption are discussed in the last section.

Preview of the answers¶

In this section, we provide an overview of our main results of the five research questions. We consider 5% as statistic significance.

1. First, we found that Covid-19 crisis significantly decreases the studying motivation of the students. The effect is approximately -2.000 points (out of 7) in the baseline OLS regression. When individual background variables are controlled, the effect is reduced to -1.984 points. However, this is still highly significant.

2. Secondly, we found no significant effect of Covid-19 crisis on the group project performance when individual background variables are controlled in the OLS regression.

3. Thirdly, we found no significant effect of Covid-19 on the exam performance of the students when individual background variables are controlled.

4. Fourthly, we found that Covid-19 significantly increases students' self-assessed loneliness. The effect is about 2.659 points (out of 7) in the baseline OLS regression. If we control for the individual background characteristics, the effect is 2.599 points, and it is still highly significant.

5. Lastly, we found that Covid-19 significantly increases students' financial stress. In the baseline OLS regression, the estimated effect is about 0.859 points (out of 7). When we control for the individual background variables, the estimated effect is 0.925 points.

More data cleaning¶

Furthermore, before running the regressions, we need to merge together the *pre*-Covid columns with the *post*-Covid ones. For example, we would like to have a dataset in which we have just one column called "Motivation", instead of "Motivation_before" and "Motivation_after". The same hold for: Group, Exam, Loneliness, Financial_stress, and Income. However, we need to create a new column called "Covid" in order to know which observations belong to the *pre*-Covid period and which ones to the *post*-Covid. This new variable should assume the value of "0" for all the *pre*-Covid observations, and "1" for all the *post*-Covid.

In the following chunks, we explain step by step how we did this. First, we created 2 copies of the original dataframe, called respectively: "df_covid_0" and "df_covid_1". However, these two are not exact copies. In fact, both have the socio-demographic characteristics, but df_covid_0 also has the *before* variables, while df_covid_1 has the *after* variables.

In [39]:
# Create a copy of df and name it df_covid_0 (socio-demographic + before-Covid variables)

                 "Income_before", "Student_finance", "Gender", "Nationality", "Second_nationality",
                 "Domicile", "Relationship_status", "Household_composition", "Education", "Major", "Second_major",
                 "University_country"]].copy()

df_covid_1 = df[["Motivation_after", "Group_after", "Exam_after", "Loneliness_after", "Financial_stress_after",
             "Income_after", "Student_finance", "Gender", "Nationality", "Second_nationality",
             "Domicile", "Relationship_status", "Household_composition", "Education", "Major", "Second_major",
             "University_country"]].copy()

Second, we create a new column called "Covid" in both the dataframes. This variable has the value of "0" for all the observations that are in df_covid_0, and the value of "1" for all the observations that are present in df_covid_1.

In [40]:

# Create the column "Covid" which is 1 for all the observations

Third, we rename the columns of both the dataframes in order to give them the same names. We want to merge them in the end, therefore this step is necessary.

Limitation and Discussion¶

The significant impact of Covid-19 on many aspects of students' life was expected, and it is difficult to avoid. Many univerisities have already made their best to continue with an effective and efficient teaching process. Regarding the loneliness and financial stress of the students, our results encourage universities and governments to provide more support for these young people. For example, universities could employ more psychologists to shorten the period of waiting for students with mental difficulties. Governments could provide more generous students finance or loans during the Covid-19 lockdown periods (although in the Netherlands, we found no significant effect of Covid-19 on financial stress of the students, so this might not applied to the Dutch government).

In the end, we stress some major limitations of our study. Due to the time limitation, we were unable to collect larger sample for higher level of accurary of the estimation. Besides, it should be noticed that our results are based on the assumption that students assessed their studying motivation, group project performance, exam performance, feeling of loneliness, and financial stress correctly. In particular, they should provide the above information about the whole period of the Covid-19 crisis during a certain point of time (the time when they fill in the survey). For example, if a student just failed one exam, he might underestimate his or her overall exam performance. The background information is also provided by students, thus we rely on the correctness of this infomation as well.

Furthermore, we use the self-assessed outcomes of the students before the Covid-19 crisis as a counterfactual to represent the expected outcomes in the state without Covid-19 crisis. However, many other external factors change during the Covid-19 crisis, compared to the time before the Covid-19 crisis, such as the university courses, and other government policies.We could not take them into account.

Future studies can go a step further, and study the impact of Covid-19 on long-term development of university students, such as the time intervel between graduation and finding the first job, and the job statisfaction of the students.
